Chairman's Synthesis

Where the room landed

A real split, not a convergence. Two members lean Candidate A (proven, low-disruption) but both attach the same condition — *unless this role needs reinvention.* One member leans Candidate B as the only path to a step-change, conditional on the org resourcing it. And two members refuse the menu: the Analyst says the decision can't be made until you answer whether this is a stabilize moment or a level-up moment, and the Steward flags that the most affected person may be an internal candidate the shortlist ignores.

Key tensions

(1) Near-term, concrete downside of a senior mis-hire (Skeptic) vs. the invisible, compounding cost of capping the team's ceiling (Strategist). (2) Talent is only fundable if you can resource it (Operator) — the bet/gamble line. (3) The whole A-vs-B framing may be wrong: there's a decisive unknown unresolved and possibly a candidate off the list.

The call

Don't choose yet — and that's not a dodge, it's the highest-value move available. Two cheap steps collapse most of the uncertainty: (1) Answer the Analyst's question explicitly — is the mandate to *stabilize* the function or *transform* it? Write it down; it largely picks the candidate. (2) Run real back-channel references and check the Steward's flag about an internal stakeholder. If it's a stabilize role and B can't be resourced, hire A with confidence. If it's a transform role and you can give B a real support structure, B is the higher-leverage bet worth the variance. The forced binary between two imperfect finalists is itself a signal that the picture isn't complete.

Pre-mortem · what this rests on

This call assumes the stabilize-vs-transform question is answerable and that the two finalists are genuinely the field. Flips if: leadership can't actually agree on the mandate (then you have an org-alignment problem, not a hiring problem), or if a strong internal candidate exists and changes the whole calculus.

Minority report · The Strategist

"I'll go on record against any default to the safe hire. Choosing the proven operator won't feel like a mistake — it never does. It will feel like prudence, and in two years you'll have a competent function that never broke out, and no one will trace it back to this room. If you can resource Candidate B at all, the asymmetry favors the bet: A's downside is a quiet ceiling, B's upside is a multiplier you cannot acquire later. Don't let 'reasonable' quietly win by forfeit."
